I finish side projects. That's not the problem. The problem is what happens
next: the repo goes private-by-default, the README trails off after the setup
section, and six months later even I can't remember what the hard parts were.
The work existed, and then it just — didn't.

Launch Start is my structural fix. Every project here runs as a **timed public
sprint**: a stated goal, a real deadline, and a log entry every week saying what
shipped, what broke, and what I learned. At the end there's a retrospective,
written while the bruises are fresh. The deadline is the point — a blog says
"I write sometimes"; a sprint says "I ship on a clock, and you can watch."

To be clear about what this isn't: it isn't a marketing funnel, and I'm not
building a personal brand with growth-hacked hooks. It's a journal. Just a guy
sharing what actually happens when you take a project from zero to shipped with
a day job in the way — including the weeks where the honest answer is "not
much, and here's why."

The first sprint is pleasingly recursive: the project is this site itself.
Thirty days to ship the platform — pages, content system, CI, deploy — with the
build documented weekly *on the thing being built*. If you're reading this, the
riskiest part already worked.

If you want to follow along, there's [RSS](/rss.xml), and each project page
keeps its full build log. The current sprint is always on the
[home page](/).
